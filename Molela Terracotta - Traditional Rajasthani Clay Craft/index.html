About Molela Terracotta

Molela terracotta is a unique form of clay craft practiced by the Kumhar (potter) community in the small village of Molela, Rajasthan. This traditional art form specializes in creating decorative clay plaques and votive tiles depicting Hindu deities, especially Devnarayan (a local folk deity), and other mythological figures.

What makes Molela terracotta distinctive is its low-relief technique and vibrant colors. The artisans create these plaques without using a potter's wheel, hand-modeling each piece from locally sourced clay. After baking, the plaques are painted in bright mineral-based colors.

Traditionally, these terracotta plaques were used in village shrines and temples, but today they have gained recognition as decorative art pieces and are collected by art enthusiasts worldwide.

History & Cultural Significance

Ancient Origins

Molela terracotta has been practiced for over 800 years, with its origins linked to the worship of Devnarayan, a local folk deity. The craft began as a way to create portable shrines for nomadic communities.

Religious Significance

Traditionally, these terracotta plaques were used in village shrines and as votive offerings. Each piece tells a story from Hindu mythology or local folklore.

Community Craft

The knowledge of this craft is passed down through generations within the Kumhar community. Entire families participate in different stages of the production process.

Modern Recognition

In recent decades, Molela terracotta has gained international recognition. Artisans have received national awards, and their work is displayed in museums worldwide.
